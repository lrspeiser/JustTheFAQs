FAQs: Harrison Ruffin Tyler

Who is Harrison Ruffin Tyler?
What is Harrison Ruffin Tyler known for?
Harrison Ruffin Tyler is a retired American chemical engineer, businessman, and preservationist, best known for cofounding ChemTreat, Inc., a water treatment company. He is also recognized for his efforts in historic preservation, particularly for Sherwood Forest Plantation and Fort Pocahontas. Tyler is one of the last surviving grandsons of the tenth U.S. President John Tyler, which links him to a significant historical legacy.
Related topics: John Tyler, Water treatment
Early Life and Education
What is significant about Harrison Ruffin Tyler's early life and education?
Born in 1928 in Virginia during the Great Depression, Tyler faced financial difficulties despite being the grandson of U.S. President John Tyler. He was homeschooled by his mother and eventually attended Charles City County public schools and the College of William & Mary, thanks to a scholarship provided by Nancy Astor. He graduated with a chemistry degree in 1949 and later studied chemical engineering at Virginia Tech due to limited job opportunities in chemistry.
Related topics: John Tyler, College of William & Mary, Virginia Polytechnic Institute and State University
Career
How did Harrison Ruffin Tyler establish his business career?
Harrison Ruffin Tyler started his career as a project manager, eventually cofounding ChemTreat, Inc. after leaving the Virginia-Carolina Chemical Corporation. His experience in water treatment, specifically dealing with soft water, led to him receiving a patent in water treatment technologies. ChemTreat became a significant player in industrial water cooling solutions for various sectors and was eventually acquired by the Danaher Corporation in 2007.
Related topics: ChemTreat, Inc., Danaher Corporation, Water treatment
Personal Life
How has Harrison Ruffin Tyler contributed to historic preservation?
Harrison Ruffin Tyler played a major role in historic preservation by purchasing the Sherwood Forest Plantation and supporting its restoration. He also funded the preservation of Fort Pocahontas, starting American Civil War reenactments at Wilson's Wharf to promote historical education. Tyler's significant donations included $5 million and numerous historical documents to the College of William & Mary's history department.
Related topics: Sherwood Forest Plantation, Fort Pocahontas, American Civil War reenactment, College of William & Mary
Legacy and Health
What challenges has Harrison Ruffin Tyler faced in his later years?
In his later years, Harrison Ruffin Tyler has experienced health challenges, including mini-strokes beginning in 2012 and developing dementia by 2020. Despite these personal challenges, he continues to have a lasting impact through his family's management of the Sherwood Forest Plantation and his contributions to historical preservation and education.
