FAQs: Auld Lang Syne

General Overview
What is the origin and significance of "Auld Lang Syne"?
"Auld Lang Syne" is a Scottish song and a poem written by Robert Burns in 1788. It is traditionally sung to bid farewell to the old year at midnight on New Year's Eve. Based on older Scottish folk songs, it was set to a traditional pentatonic tune in 1799, which has since become the standard melody. The phrase "Auld Lang Syne" translates to "old long since," or more colloquially, "long long ago," "days gone by," or "times long past." It symbolizes a call to remember long-standing friendships and is often used at funerals, graduations, and Scout jamborees.

Lyrics
What are the key themes depicted in the lyrics of "Auld Lang Syne"?
The lyrics of "Auld Lang Syne" begin with a rhetorical question, "Should auld acquaintance be forgot, and never brought to mind?" This reflects on the preservation of old friendships and the sharing of past memories. The chorus reinforces the sentiment by proposing a toast 'for auld lang syne', celebrating the times gone by. Traditionally, the song captures nostalgic and celebratory themes, urging people to remember cherished moments and friends from the past.

History
Who originally composed "Auld Lang Syne", and how did it become popular?
Although Robert Burns is credited with writing "Auld Lang Syne" in 1788 by sending it to the Scots Musical Museum, it was based on a traditional folk tune. Burns claimed it was an old song never before printed. The version of the melody universally known today was first published in 1799 in George Thomson's "Select Songs of Scotland". The tune is believed to be a pentatonic Scots melody that was originally a quicker dance-tempo.
Related topics: Robert Burns, George Thomson (musician), Scots Musical Museum
Settings and Quotations of the Melody
How has the melody of "Auld Lang Syne" been used in classical and popular music?
The melody of "Auld Lang Syne" has been arranged by several classical composers, including Joseph Haydn and Ludwig van Beethoven, who helped to popularize the song. It has also been quoted by composers like William Shield and George M. Cohan. Cohan used it deliberately in his song, "You're a Grand Old Flag." The tune's adaptability has allowed it to be used in diverse ways across different musical genres and compositions, from classical to marching music.
Related topics: Joseph Haydn, Ludwig van Beethoven, George M. Cohan, William Shield
When Sung
In what occasions, other than New Year, is "Auld Lang Syne" commonly sung?
Aside from being a New Year's Eve anthem, "Auld Lang Syne" is often sung at occasions marking endings and new beginnings, such as funerals, graduations, Scout jamborees, and at the closing of parties. It has been used to mark significant historical events like the handover ceremonies of former British colonies.
Related topics: New Year's Eve, Hogmanay, Scouting, Funerals
