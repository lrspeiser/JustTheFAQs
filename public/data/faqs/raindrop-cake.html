FAQs: Raindrop Cake

Overview
What is a raindrop cake?
A raindrop cake is a Japanese wagashi dessert made primarily of water and agar, designed to resemble a large raindrop. It's a confection that gained popularity in Japan in 2014 and later captured international interest due to its unique appearance and near-zero calories.
Related topics: Wagashi, Confectionery
History
How did the raindrop cake originate?
In Japan, the dessert is known as 'mizu shingen mochi', an evolution of the traditional Japanese shingen mochi cake. Originating in the city of Yamanashi, it was inspired by abekawa mochi and made by Kinseiken, a company looking to create 'edible water'. The dessert soared to fame as a viral sensation in 2014.
Related topics: Shingen mochi, Abekawa mochi, Obon Festival, Yamanashi Prefecture
Evolution and Spread
How did the raindrop cake make its way to the United States?
Darren Wong introduced the raindrop cake to the United States in April 2016 at the Smorgasburg food fair in New York City. Its whimsical presentation helped it gain quick attention and popularity.
Related topics: Smorgasburg
Description
What are the main ingredients and characteristics of the raindrop cake?
The raindrop cake is made from mineral water and agar, a vegan seaweed-based gelatin alternative, with no calories. The original cake is sourced from the waters of Mount Kaikoma in Japan's Southern Alps, and it offers a delicate, mildly sweet taste. Toppings include kuromitsu (a molasses-like syrup) and kinako (roasted soybean flour).
Related topics: Mineral water, Agar, Mount Kaikoma, Kuromitsu, Kinako
Culinary Characteristics
What makes the raindrop cake unique in terms of its culinary appeal?
The raindrop cake is unique due to its transparent, water droplet-like appearance and its ephemeral nature. It's often compared to jellyfish or breast implants visually, and it must be consumed quickly as it evaporates within twenty to thirty minutes of serving.
Related topics: Evaporation
Cultural Significance
What is the cultural background of the raindrop cake?
The raindrop cake draws from the cultural heritage of Japanese mochi-like sweets, reflecting the simplicity and elegance found in Japanese confections. Its original form, mizu shingen mochi, is still produced in the Yamanashi area, celebrated for its pure water sourced from nearby mountains.
