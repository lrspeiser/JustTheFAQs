FAQs: Twelve Grapes

Global Influence
Is the Twelve Grapes tradition observed outside of Spain?
Yes, the Twelve Grapes tradition has been embraced in countries with cultural connections to Spain, including the Philippines, many Latin American countries, and among Hispanic communities in the United States. It is a significant component of Hispanic Christmas celebrations and is also practiced by some Jamaicans.
Related topics: Philippines, Christmas, Jamaicans
Overview
What is the Twelve Grapes tradition?
The Twelve Grapes is a Spanish tradition where each of the twelve clock bell strikes at midnight on December 31st is marked by consuming a grape. This ritual is believed to bring luck and prosperity for the twelve months of the coming year. The tradition originated to boost grape sales following an excellent harvest.

Celebration
Where is the most famous place to celebrate the Twelve Grapes in Spain?
The most famous place to celebrate the Twelve Grapes is at the Puerta del Sol in Madrid, in front of the Royal House of the Post Office clock tower. This location holds historical significance as the starting point of the tradition, which is broadcast nationwide every year.
Related topics: Puerta del Sol, Madrid, Royal House of the Post Office
History
How did the Twelve Grapes tradition begin?
The Twelve Grapes tradition can be traced back to at least 1895. It became widely popular in 1909 when vine growers in the Province of Alicante promoted this tradition to deal with grape overproduction. It was believed to bring good luck and ward off misfortune, although today it is mainly a festive activity.
