FAQs: Gregorian calendar

Introduction
What is the Gregorian calendar, and why is it significant?
The Gregorian calendar is the internationally accepted civil calendar, used in most parts of the world. It was introduced as a reform of the Julian calendar by a commission assembled by Pope Gregory XIII in 1582. This calendar closely approximates the 365.2422-day tropical or solar year determined by Earth's revolution around the Sun. The reform adjusted the leap year system and corrected the drift of equinoxes to better align with the solar year.
Related topics: Calendar, Julian calendar, Tropical year
Gregorian Reform
Who were the key figures behind the Gregorian calendar reform, and what changes did they implement?
The key figures behind the Gregorian calendar reform included Pope Gregory XIII and Christopher Clavius, an influential astronomer. The reform adjusted the computation of leap years to better approximate the tropical year, advanced the date of the March equinox by 10 days, and altered the lunar cycle for calculating Easter. This ensured that the equinox and the calendar year would remain in closer synchronization over the centuries.
Related topics: Christopher Clavius, Pope Gregory XIII, Inter gravissimas
Adoption and Impact
How was the Gregorian calendar adopted worldwide?
Initially, the Gregorian calendar was adopted by Catholic Europe and its colonies in 1582. Protestant and Eastern Orthodox countries gradually adopted it as well, though some Orthodox churches continued to use the Julian calendar for religious purposes. Great Britain and its colonies adopted the calendar in 1752, and most non-Western countries followed it in the 20th century for civil purposes.

Detailed Mechanics
How does the Gregorian calendar define leap years?
In the Gregorian calendar, a leap year is any year that is exactly divisible by four, except for years that are exactly divisible by 100. However, century years are leap years if they are divisible by 400. This rule aligns the calendar year with the tropical year more closely than the Julian system, which had a leap year every four years without exception.
Related topics: Leap year, Julian calendar
Differences from the Julian Calendar
What are the major differences between the Gregorian and Julian calendars?
The major difference between the Gregorian and Julian calendars is the method of calculating leap years. The Julian calendar added a leap day every four years, which was too frequent, causing significant drift over time. The Gregorian calendar refines this by making century years non-leap years unless divisible by 400, thereby reducing the leap years from 100 to 97 every 400 years.
Related topics: Julian calendar, Leap year
Cultural and Religious Significance
How does the Gregorian calendar affect the celebration of Easter?
The Gregorian calendar affects Easter by stabilizing the calculation of the March equinox, which is fundamental to determining Easter's date. The reforms ensured that this equinox remained close to its intended date to prevent drift that had earlier caused misalignment of Easter with the spring equinox, critical for Christian liturgical timing.
Related topics: Date of Easter, Equinox
Timeline of Adoption
Which countries were the last to adopt the Gregorian calendar and when?
Greece was the last European country to adopt the Gregorian calendar for civil use in 1923. Many non-Western countries adopted it later, with Turkey extending its use to general purposes in 1926. However, some Orthodox churches continue to use the Julian calendar for religious purposes.
Related topics: Adoption of the Gregorian calendar, Julian calendar
Proposed Reforms
Are there any proposed reforms to the Gregorian calendar, and what do they entail?
There have been several proposed reforms to the Gregorian calendar aimed at simplifying or rearranging the calendar year. Notable proposals include the International Fixed Calendar, the World Calendar, and Leap Week calendars like Symmetry454, each proposing different methods to organize days and weeks consistently across years.
Related topics: Calendar reform, International Fixed Calendar, World Calendar
