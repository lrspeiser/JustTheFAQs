FAQs: Lyon Gardiner Tyler

Early Life and Education
What was Tyler's early life and educational background?
Tyler was born in Charles City County, Virginia, at his father's Sherwood Forest Plantation. He was the fourth son of President John Tyler. During the American Civil War, his family moved to Staten Island for safety. After the war, Tyler returned to Virginia to study at the University of Virginia, where he earned bachelor's and master's degrees in law in 1875.
Related topics: John Tyler, Sherwood Forest Plantation, University of Virginia
Personal Life
Who were Lyon Gardiner Tyler's family members?
Tyler married twice, first to Anne Baker Tucker and later to Sue Ruffin. He had six children, including Harrison Ruffin Tyler. His family has played a prominent role in historical preservation, with his descendant Harrison contributing significantly to the College of William & Mary.
Related topics: Harrison Ruffin Tyler, College of William & Mary

Who was Lyon Gardiner Tyler?

Tyler served as the 17th president of the College of William & Mary, was an advocate for historical research and preservation, and a vocal critic of U.S. President Abraham Lincoln.

Death and Legacy
What is Tyler's legacy after his death?
Tyler died in 1935 and is buried in Hollywood Cemetery, Richmond. His contributions to historical research are remembered, but his legacy has been reassessed, leading to renaming of institutions formerly named in his honor due to his views supporting racial segregation. His major works include a variety of historical and genealogical publications.
Related topics: Hollywood Cemetery (Richmond, Virginia), William and Mary College
Political and Academic Career
What roles did Tyler play in education and politics?
Tyler initially taught at the College of William & Mary before moving to Memphis, Tennessee. After studying law and returning to Richmond, he became involved in politics, serving in the Virginia House of Delegates. He played a significant role in securing funding to reopen the College of William & Mary, where he later became president.
Related topics: College of William & Mary, Virginia House of Delegates
Criticism and Later Life
How did Tyler criticize President Lincoln, and what was his life like post-retirement?
Later in life, Tyler became known for his criticism of President Abraham Lincoln, often drawing controversial parallels in his writings. After resigning as president of the College of William & Mary in 1919, he focused on writing and preserving Virginia's history. His pamphlets and articles frequently criticized Lincoln and supported Confederate views.
