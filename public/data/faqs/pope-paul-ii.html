FAQs: Pope Paul II

Introduction
Who was Pope Paul II?
Pope Paul II, originally named Pietro Barbo, was the head of the Catholic Church from August 30, 1464, until his death on July 26, 1471. He was noted for amassing a great collection of art and antiquities during his papacy.
Related topics: Pope John Paul II
Early Life
How did Pope Paul II begin his career in the Church?
Pope Paul II, born Pietro Barbo, initially trained to be a merchant. However, his career direction changed when his maternal uncle was elected Pope Eugene IV, inspiring Barbo to pursue a religious path. He rose quickly in the Church hierarchy, becoming a cardinal-deacon in 1440.
Related topics: Bishop of Rome
Election
How did Pope Paul II become pope?
Paul II was elected as pope in the papal conclave of August 30, 1464, by the 'accessus' method, succeeding Pope Pius II. His election was partly due to the dissatisfaction of cardinals with his predecessor's policy.
Related topics: 1464 papal conclave, Pope Pius II
Conflict
What conflicts did Pope Paul II face during his papacy?
Pope Paul II faced several conflicts, including tensions with cardinals over the creation of new cardinals and opposition from humanists due to his annulment of the College of Abbreviators, who were responsible for formulating papal documents. He also arrested members of the Roman Academy, like Bartolomeo Platina, due to alleged conspiracies against him.
Related topics: Bartolomeo Platina, Renaissance humanism
Final Years
What led to Pope Paul II's death?
Pope Paul II's death on July 26, 1471, is subject to various accounts. Some claim he died from a heart attack due to severe indigestion after eating melon excessively, while others suggest more scandalous circumstances. His death led to a power vacuum in Central Europe.
Related topics: Excommunication, Sodomized
Legacy
What is Pope Paul II's legacy in the Church?
Despite being an opponent of humanist learning, Pope Paul II facilitated the introduction of printing into the Papal States, making books more accessible and affordable. He also allowed for popular amusements and attempted to reduce blood feuds in Italy. His efforts included treating Jews fairly and accumulating a massive treasure for protection against the Turks.
Related topics: Renaissance humanism, Printing, Papal States
