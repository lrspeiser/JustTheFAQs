FAQs: Houthi movement

Overview
What is the Houthi movement?
The Houthi movement, officially known as Ansar Allah, is a Shia Islamist political and military organization originating in Yemen. It is primarily composed of Zaydi Shias, led by the Houthi tribe. The group has been a key player in Yemen's civil war, known for human rights abuses and the use of child soldiers. It is designated as a terrorist organization by several countries.
Related topics: Yemeni civil war (2014–present), Human rights
Leadership
Who are the leaders of the Houthi movement?
The movement has been led by Abdul-Malik al-Houthi since 2004 after the death of its initial leader, Hussein al-Houthi. Other significant leaders include Yahia Badreddin al-Houthi, Abdul-Karim Badreddin al-Houthi, and military commander, Abu Ali Abdullah al-Hakem.
Related topics: Abdul-Malik al-Houthi, Hussein al-Houthi
Historical Context
How did the Houthi movement begin?
The Houthi movement began as a moderate theological movement in the 1990s called "the Believing Youth," founded in Saada Governorate to promote a Zaydi Shia revival. It transformed into a political and military movement under Hussein al-Houthi, opposing the Yemeni government and foreign influence.
Related topics: History of Yemen, Saada Governorate
Ideology
What does the ideology of the Houthi movement entail?
The Houthi movement propagates a blend of Shia Islamism, Yemeni nationalism, and populism, portraying themselves as defenders of Yemen against external influences. Their ideology includes the 'Quranic Way' and supports governance by the Prophet’s descendants.
Related topics: Shia Islamism, Yemeni nationalism
Current Affairs and Conflicts
What is the Houthi movement's role in the Yemeni Civil War?
The Houthi movement is a major faction in the Yemeni Civil War, initially opposing Yemen's government before capturing the capital, Sanaa, in 2014. They have since fought against a Saudi-led coalition supporting the Yemeni government in a conflict involving regional and international actors.
Related topics: Yemeni Civil War, Saudi-led intervention in the Yemeni civil war
Human Rights Violations
What human rights violations have been attributed to the Houthi movement?
The Houthi movement has been accused of numerous human rights violations, including the use of child soldiers, hostage-taking, and the diversion of international aid. Reports also highlight abuses against women and minority communities such as the Baháʼís and Jews in Yemen.
Related topics: Human rights, Baháʼí Faith
International Relations
What international support or alliances does the Houthi movement have?
The Houthis are alleged to receive support from Iran, including training and weaponry. They reportedly have ties with non-state actors like Hezbollah. However, these relationships are often denied by the parties involved.
Related topics: Iran–Saudi Arabia proxy conflict, Hezbollah
Involvement in Other Conflicts
How is the Houthi movement involved in other regional conflicts?
The Houthis have extended their involvement beyond Yemen, notably participating in the Israel–Hamas war of 2023 by firing missiles at Israel as an expression of solidarity with the Palestinians.
Related topics: Israel–Hamas war
Domestic Impact and Governance
How do the Houthis govern areas under their control?
In regions they control, particularly in Northern Yemen, the Houthis implement their own legal system, often seeking to provide security and resolve disputes in areas neglected by the Yemeni government. However, their governance is reported to include arbitrary detentions and imposition of sectarian beliefs.
Related topics: Houthi-controlled areas of Yemen
Military Strength
What is the military capability of the Houthi movement?
The Houthi movement has developed a substantial military capability, deploying advanced weaponry like ballistic missiles and anti-ship capabilities. Their forces are reportedly well-armed with equipment seized from Yemeni military stocks and have been known to receive external support, notably from Iran.
Related topics: Houthi insurgency in Yemen
